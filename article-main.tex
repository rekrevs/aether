\begin{abstract}
We ask whether an underlying discrete substrate with a global update ordering could support an additional influence channel, based on pattern similarity rather than spacetime proximity, that appears superluminal in an emergent relativistic spacetime while remaining compatible with standard low-energy physics. To address this we construct an explicit effective field theory in which ordinary fields on an emergent manifold are coupled to an internal “pattern space” that measures structural similarity of local configurations. A weak, substrate-local interaction in this space (“aether resonance”) redistributes energy and information between similar patterns and projects as apparently FTL transfer in spacetime.

The construction is intended as an existence proof for a broader design space of pattern-local, globally time-ordered channels. We identify a small set of structural requirements—locality in pattern space with finite propagation speed, a globally monotone substrate time, and covariant exchange of stress–energy—and show that they force several nontrivial constraints: the extra sector must gravitate with the same strength as visible matter; FTL signalling in spacetime cannot be arranged into antitelephone protocols or closed causal loops; and any usable channel is bounded by a thermodynamic resource inequality and a modified Lieb–Robinson “soft cone” outside the usual light cone. We then map the resulting parameter space to existing Lorentz-violation bounds and outline three concrete experimental families that could either significantly tighten those bounds for this whole class of models or reveal a reproducible, pattern-dependent superluminal channel.
\end{abstract}
